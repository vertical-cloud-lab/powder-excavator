# State of the Art: Generative Computational CAD and Closed-Loop Feedback for an Open-Hardware Powder-Excavator

## 1. Generative / Computational CAD and Systems Design for Mechanical Hardware

### 1.1 Parametric / Algorithmic CAD Ecosystems

The landscape of code-first parametric CAD tools relevant to open-source mechanical hardware has matured considerably. **CadQuery 2** stands as the strongest candidate for a pip-installable, open, Python-native parametric CAD authoring layer. Built on OpenCASCADE Technology (OCCT) via Python bindings, CadQuery produces true boundary-representation (B-rep) solids with native STEP export, full Python library integration, and a concise workplane-centric API well-suited to fixtures, cams, slots, and print-ready lab hardware (machado2019parametriccadmodeling pages 4-5, machado2019parametriccadmodeling pages 15-16). Machado et al. (2019) systematically compared OpenSCAD and FreeCAD Python for open-source scientific hardware and concluded that while OpenSCAD is easy to learn and widely used, it is fundamentally limited by its tessellated/polygonal kernel (CGAL-based CSG), which cannot export to standard parametric formats and loses dimensional parametric information (machado2019parametriccadmodeling pages 25-26, machado2019parametriccadmodeling pages 12-15, machado2019parametriccadmodeling pages 2-4). FreeCAD Python, sharing CadQuery's OCCT kernel, offers GUI integration and parametric STEP export but suffers from a steeper learning curve and less streamlined scripting workflow (machado2019parametriccadmodeling pages 25-26, machado2019parametriccadmodeling pages 15-16). The CadQuery workbench within FreeCAD was noted to ameliorate several FreeCAD usability negatives (machado2019parametriccadmodeling pages 26-28). **build123d** is a newer Apache-2.0 Python library also wrapping OCCT, offering a more explicit sketch/part/assembly flow that may appeal to users who find CadQuery's selector idioms opaque, though it has a smaller ecosystem and fewer published scientific-hardware exemplars. Commercial tools—Rhino + Grasshopper (visual parametric graph), Onshape FeatureScript (cloud SaaS), and Zoo Design Studio / KCL (purpose-built text-first design language)—offer strong capabilities but do not meet the open-source, pip-installable, no-license-fee constraint.

### 1.2 Generative / AI-Assisted Geometry

Deep generative models for CAD have evolved rapidly. **DeepCAD** pioneered treating CAD construction as a sequence of parametric commands (line, arc, circle, extrude) modeled by a Transformer autoencoder, training on 178,238 models (xie2505texttocadqueryanew pages 12-15). However, its fixed command vocabulary and quantized parameter representation limit it to relatively simple sketch-and-extrude parts, distant from the nuanced geometry of cam profiles, compliant flexures, or passive-tilt mechanisms.

**BrepGen** advanced the field by using diffusion models over structured latent B-rep trees, directly generating boundary representations including free-form and doubly-curved surfaces for the first time (xu2024brepgenabrep pages 3-4, xu2024brepgenabrep pages 2-3, xu2024brepgenabrep pages 1-2). BrepGen supports design interpolation, autocomplete, and inpainting-like completion in CAD-native form, with code, pretrained models, and datasets released (xu2024brepgenabrep pages 1-2). However, unconditional topology generation remains challenging, with non-watertight solids, self-intersections, and low validity ratios still problematic (xu2024brepgenabrep pages 9-11).

The most practically relevant approach for an open CadQuery-centered workflow is **Text-to-CadQuery**, which fine-tunes pretrained LLMs to generate executable CadQuery Python code directly from natural-language descriptions (xie2505texttocadqueryanew pages 1-3, xie2505texttocadqueryanew pages 3-4). By bypassing intermediate CAD-token representations and leveraging models' existing Python-generation and spatial-reasoning strengths, this approach achieved a top-1 exact match of 69.3% and reduced Chamfer Distance by 48.6% relative to Text2CAD baselines (xie2505texttocadqueryanew pages 8-10). The output is interpretable, editable Python source that integrates directly into an existing CadQuery workflow. Mesh-only generators like **Shap-E** and **TRELLIS** produce visually impressive 3D content but output non-parametric meshes lacking the constraint-driven, dimensioned structure needed for toleranced mechanical parts. Commercial tools like **nTop** (field-driven implicit modeling) and **Fusion Generative Design** (topology optimization) are powerful but proprietary and not aligned with a no-license-fee open build.

### 1.3 Systems Design and Requirements-Driven Approaches

**OpenMDAO** is the leading open-source Python framework for multidisciplinary design, analysis, and optimization (MDO). It provides a modular, component-based architecture where disciplinary analyses are wired together with automated data-passing and derivative computation (gray2019openmdaoanopensource pages 20-22, gray2019openmdaoanopensource pages 22-23, gray2019openmdaoanopensource pages 2-4). OpenMDAO supports gradient-based optimization with efficient analytic adjoint methods, parallel computation on distributed systems, and has been used for large problems with thousands of variables (gray2019openmdaoanopensource pages 22-23). The framework is extensible via plugins and integrates with SciPy optimizers, surrogate-based approaches including Kriging/Expected Improvement (gray2010openmdaoanopen pages 1-3), and external solvers. For the powder-excavator, OpenMDAO could wire together a CadQuery geometry generator, a kinematic model of the passive-tilt cam schedule, a slicing/DFM check, and a physics-based powder-flow estimator into a single optimization problem.

**SysML v2** provides a standardized modeling language for systems requirements and architecture but lacks a pip-installable open implementation suitable for tight integration with Python CAD workflows. For Bayesian optimization specifically, **BoTorch** (BSD-3) and its higher-level wrapper **Ax** (MIT) provide state-of-the-art Gaussian-process-based acquisition functions (Expected Improvement, Knowledge Gradient, multi-objective EHVI) that integrate naturally with Python and can drive parametric design exploration over CadQuery-generated geometries.

### 1.4 Automated DFM / Printability Checks

Automated design-for-manufacturing (DFM) checking sits at three tiers. **Slicer-side checks** (PrusaSlicer, OrcaSlicer, Cura) are the most accessible open layer: command-line invocation produces support-volume estimates, wall-thickness warnings, overhang analysis, and print-time/material estimates. **DFMPro** and **Castor** offer more comprehensive DFM rule engines (minimum wall, draft, undercut, feature-accessibility) but are proprietary and license-fee-based. For the powder-excavator, the recommended approach is to combine PrusaSlicer CLI automation with custom CadQuery-side geometric validators that check bucket clearance, cam contact width, minimum flexure thickness, trapped-powder volumes, and gantry-reachable assembly constraints—all expressible as Python functions operating on the CadQuery B-rep model before export.

## 2. Automated Feedback Loops and Digital-Twin-Style Evaluation

### 2.1 Digital Twin Maturity

The widely cited Kritzinger et al. (2018) taxonomy distinguishes three levels of cyber-physical coupling: a **Digital Model** with only manual data exchange, a **Digital Shadow** with automated one-way data flow from physical to virtual, and a true **Digital Twin** with fully automated bi-directional synchronization enabling active control feedback (lattanzi2021digitaltwinfor pages 6-9, lattanzi2021digitaltwinfor pages 9-11). Grieves' foundational definition frames the twin as three parts—physical product, virtual product, and the data connections bridging them—spanning the entire product lifecycle (nguyen2025aidrivendigitaltwins pages 2-4). For the powder-excavator, the pragmatic design target is to begin as a Digital Model (CadQuery geometry + kinematic simulation), progress to a Digital Shadow (automated sensor logging from balance + vision to a data store), and ultimately achieve a Digital Twin where measured performance automatically triggers parameter updates and re-design.

### 2.2 AM-Specific Twins and Open Platforms

NIST AM-Bench provides standardized benchmark data for validating AM simulation, monitoring, and prediction pipelines—a model for creating a "powder-bench" with standard scoop/dispense artifacts, powders, camera views, and balance protocols. **Eclipse Ditto** offers an open-source digital-twin middleware for IoT-connected devices, suitable as a state-management layer. **ROS2 + Gazebo/Ignition** provides strong open-source robotics simulation for motion logic, toolpath sequencing, sensor fusion, and virtual commissioning of gantry systems, though it lacks built-in granular-powder physics and must be paired with empirical powder models.

### 2.3 Closed-Loop Design → Print → Measure → Re-Design Pipelines

The **AM ARES** system (Deneault et al., 2021) is the closest architectural precedent to what the powder-excavator requires. AM ARES implements a fully autonomous closed-loop pipeline: a material-extrusion printer with integrated cameras captures images after each print, automated image analysis scores the result, and an online Bayesian optimizer proposes the next parameter set (deneault2021towardautonomousadditive pages 2-3, deneault2021towardautonomousadditive pages 7-8, deneault2021towardautonomousadditive pages 1-2). The system learned to produce target single-layer features in fewer than 100 experiments and achieved objective scores of ~0.94 with four-parameter tuning in under three hours (deneault2021towardautonomousadditive pages 7-8, deneault2021towardautonomousadditive pages 1-2). The ARES OS software and low-cost hardware designs are intended for open-source release (deneault2021towardautonomousadditive pages 1-2).

The **A-Lab** (Szymanski et al., 2023) demonstrated a fully autonomous solid-state inorganic-powder synthesis laboratory that ran 355 experiments over 17 days, realizing 41 of 58 novel target compounds (~71% success rate) through an active-learning loop integrating ML-proposed synthesis recipes, robotic powder dosing and heating, automated XRD characterization, and thermodynamics-grounded recipe refinement (szymanski2023anautonomouslaboratory pages 1-2, szymanski2023anautonomouslaboratory pages 4-5). Its precedent is directly relevant to powder handling: failed trials are treated as informative and feed updated parameters rather than simple pass/fail outcomes.

Multi-objective Bayesian optimization for material extrusion has been demonstrated in an initialize→plan→experiment→analyze→conclude loop on low-cost open syringe-extrusion systems, extracting Pareto trade-offs between competing printing objectives (myung2025multiobjectivebayesianoptimization pages 1-2). This is highly relevant because the powder-excavator has inherently conflicting objectives: dose accuracy, throughput, powder disturbance, carry-back contamination, and mechanical robustness.

### 2.4 In-Situ AM Monitoring and CV-Based Metrology

In-situ monitoring of AM processes has advanced through melt-pool sensing (thermal cameras, pyrometers), optical coherence tomography (OCT), and increasingly through computer-vision (CV) defect detection. Consumer-facing tools like PrintWatch and The Spaghetti Detective use webcam-based CNNs for real-time failure detection (stringing, spaghetti, layer delamination). For the powder-excavator, a simpler CV pipeline suffices: top-down imaging to detect powder spills, measure heap profiles, verify bucket return-to-home, and estimate dose completeness. OpenCV (Apache-2.0, pip-installable) provides the necessary image processing, contour detection, and calibration routines.

### 2.5 The Jubilee / Science-Jubilee Open-Hardware Platform

**Jubilee** is an open-source, tool-changing motion platform (CC BY 4.0 hardware, MIT software) that supports automatic tool exchange, modular end-effectors (cameras, syringes, pipette adapters, probes), and programmable lab automation from Jupyter notebooks via the **science_jubilee** Python library (subbaraman2024theduckbota pages 7-9, pelkie2025democratizingselfdrivinglabs pages 9-11, subbaraman2024theduckbota pages 5-7, vasquez2020jubileeanextensible pages 7-9). The platform uses a Duet3 motion controller, communicates over USB serial or Ethernet, and supports OpenTrons labware definitions (subbaraman2024theduckbota pages 5-7). It has been used for multi-step workflows including imaging, liquid handling, and materials characterization (pelkie2025democratizingselfdrivinglabs pages 9-11, pelkie2025democratizingselfdrivinglabs pages 3-6). At approximately $2,000 and ~100 hours to reproduce (pelkie2025democratizingselfdrivinglabs pages 3-6), Jubilee provides the strongest open-hardware base for the powder-excavator test rig.

### 2.6 A Credible End-to-End Loop for the Powder-Excavator

Drawing on these precedents, a credible end-to-end loop for the powder-excavator would proceed as follows:

1. **Parametric CAD generation (CadQuery):** A Python script parameterizes the bucket profile, cam ramp geometry, compliant-flexure dimensions, and passive-tilt schedule as a function of gantry position. Parameters include scoop depth, entry angle, cam rise/dwell/fall profile, flexure stiffness, and dump clearance.

2. **DFM validation:** PrusaSlicer CLI checks printability (minimum wall, overhang, support volume); custom CadQuery validators check bucket–gantry clearance, cam contact pressure, and flexure fatigue life.

3. **Print:** Automated G-code generation and print dispatch to an FDM printer (ideally on the same Jubilee gantry or a dedicated printer with serial control).

4. **Physical testing on Jubilee rig:** The gantry runs the bucket through its programmed tilt schedule over a powder bed. A balance (USB-serial scale) measures dispensed mass per cycle. A top-down camera (tool-mounted on Jubilee) captures images for spill detection, heap-profile measurement, and bucket-state verification via OpenCV.

5. **Bayesian optimizer (BoTorch/Ax):** Multi-objective acquisition functions (e.g., EHVI) balance dose accuracy (mass variance), throughput (cycle time), and contamination (carry-back mass). The optimizer proposes the next set of CadQuery parameters.

6. **Orchestration:** A lightweight workflow manager (Prefect or Luigi) chains CAD generation → slicing → printing → testing → optimization → logging. Jupyter notebooks serve for exploratory development; Git tracks parametric design history and experiment provenance.

This architecture starts as a Digital Shadow (automated one-way data logging) and becomes a true Digital Twin when the optimizer's proposed parameters automatically trigger re-generation and re-printing without human intervention—matching the Kritzinger bi-directional criterion (lattanzi2021digitaltwinfor pages 6-9).
