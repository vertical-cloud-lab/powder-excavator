## 1. Introduction

The design of even a modest mechanical device—a passive, gantry-mounted powder-excavator trough that scoops and tips dozens-of-micron inorganic powders—can benefit enormously from the computational design, AI-assisted geometry, and closed-loop experimental infrastructure that have matured over the past five years. This literature synthesis surveys two intertwined frontiers: (i) the state of the art in generative and computational CAD for mechanical-engineering systems design, and (ii) the emerging practice of automated feedback loops and digital-twin-style evaluation for additively manufactured hardware prototypes. We conclude with concrete toolchain recommendations for the powder-excavator project.

## 2. Topic 1 — Generative and Computational CAD for Systems Design

### 2.1 Parametric and Algorithmic CAD Ecosystems (2024–2026)

**Visual-programming platforms.** Grasshopper, the visual algorithm editor embedded in Rhinoceros 3D, remains the dominant environment for parametric geometry in architecture, engineering, and fabrication. Recent work demonstrates its capacity to automate generation of product-platform variants and to integrate solver plug-ins such as Kangaroo (live physics/form-finding), Karamba3D (parametric FEA), and Galapagos (evolutionary optimisation) directly within the visual-programming canvas (oliva2020computinginthe pages 36-45, martinmariscal2025grasshopperalgorithmicmodelling pages 18-20, martinmariscal2025grasshopperalgorithmicmodelling pages 1-2). Grasshopper's strengths lie in managing and adjusting geometric design parameters, systematically generating product variants, and supporting high customisation while maintaining platform coherence (martinmariscal2025grasshopperalgorithmicmodelling pages 18-20). However, complex platform projects with many variants, conditional geometries, or compatibility checks require scripting in Python or C# to add conditionals, loops, and access to external data sources (martinmariscal2025grasshopperalgorithmicmodelling pages 18-20). The newer Rhino.Inside and Rhino.Compute pipelines enable headless and cloud execution, making Grasshopper definitions callable from Python notebooks and CI systems. Dynamo for Revit occupies a parallel niche in the AEC/BIM domain, exposing the Revit API via node-based graphs and enabling parametric automation of building models (oliva2020computinginthe pages 36-45). **Code-CAD.** For automation-first workflows, code-CAD tools offer superior version-control diffability, reproducibility, and integration into optimisation loops. OpenSCAD (GPL-2, ~14 years mature) provides a declarative CSG scripting language atop the CGAL kernel, producing STL/AMF/3MF outputs; it is widely used for simple parametric parts but lacks native B-rep support and robust assembly semantics. CadQuery (~8 years, Apache-2.0) and the newer build123d (~2 years, Apache-2.0) both wrap the OpenCascade (OCP) kernel from Python, producing native STEP and BREP outputs with full B-rep solid modelling. Both integrate well with Jupyter notebooks and are thus natural substrates for LLM-driven CAD scripting and optimisation loops (guan2025cadcodertexttocadgeneration pages 14-16, doris2026cadcoderanopensource pages 2-3). The CAD-Coder family of systems has demonstrated that fine-tuned vision-language models can generate executable CadQuery Python code with 100% valid syntax rates (doris2026cadcoderanopensource pages 2-3). Additional code-CAD entrants include Zoo Design Studio/KCL (Rust-based, functional CAD language), Replicad (TypeScript wrapper around OpenCascade), and Manifold (a high-performance mesh-boolean library). Cloud-native parametric CAD platforms such as Onshape (FeatureScript), Fusion 360 (Python API + Forma), and Shapr3D provide browser-accessible parametric modelling with API endpoints but impose subscription costs and vendor lock-in that complicate headless automation.

### 2.2 Generative and AI-Assisted Geometry Creation

**Topology optimisation in commercial CAE.** Commercial tools including nTopology, Altair Inspire/OptiStruct, Ansys Discovery, Autodesk Fusion Generative Design, and Siemens NX offer topology optimisation (TO) and generative design (GD) workflows that accept load cases, boundary conditions, and material properties and produce optimised material distributions or multiple design candidates (li2024collaborativemodelbasedsystems pages 19-21, stirgwolt2022amodelbasedsystems pages 4-6). Barbieri & Muzzupappa (2022) and others have compared TO and GD in the AM context, finding that both can be adopted early in a design process to make parts lighter and stronger, but that they differ in output: TO typically uses density-based SIMP algorithms producing intermediate-density distributions that require conversion to manufacturable geometry, while GD produces a population of variants on a Pareto front that requires designer filtering and selection (marvis2022modelbasedsystemsengineering pages 41-45). Practical limitations include weak integration of AM-specific constraints, unclear conversion from density fields to printable geometry, and the persistent need for human interpretation and validation (marvis2022modelbasedsystemsengineering pages 41-45, hoepfner2023towardsaholistic pages 9-9). Current software often falls short in supporting the unique requirements of AM, such as graded materials and multi-functional designs (li2024collaborativemodelbasedsystems pages 1-3).

**ML-for-CAD research.** A rapidly growing body of work applies deep generative models to CAD. DeepCAD introduced unconditional generation of parametric sketch-and-extrude command sequences, but is limited to simple geometries because its training dataset is heavily biased toward low-complexity models (yu2026gencadthreedimensionalcomputeraideddesign pages 1-2). BrepGen targets direct B-rep generation via diffusion models, while CAD-Recode reverse-engineers CadQuery code from point clouds (lu2601flattenthecomplex pages 7-8, yu2026gencadthreedimensionalcomputeraideddesign pages 15-15). GenCAD-3D combines contrastive learning for multimodal latent-space alignment with synthetic data augmentation to improve reconstruction of complex CAD geometries (yu2026gencadthreedimensionalcomputeraideddesign pages 14-15, yu2026gencadthreedimensionalcomputeraideddesign pages 1-2). However, many models trained on such data perform well on simple shapes but struggle on intricate, high-complexity geometries, and generated programs can require multiple iterations of validation and re-generation before producing valid structures (chen2026learningfromdesign pages 9-11, yu2026gencadthreedimensionalcomputeraideddesign pages 1-2). Only models that explicitly generate B-reps are likely to produce geometry approaching engineering grade; sequence-, sketch-, mesh-, or point-cloud-based outputs typically require topology repair, parameterisation, and feature/constraint recovery before meeting engineering CAD requirements (yu2026gencadthreedimensionalcomputeraideddesign pages 15-15).

**LLM-driven CAD scripting agents.** The intersection of large language models and parametric CAD scripting is a particularly active area. Zhang et al. (2026) provide the first systematic survey of LLMs for CAD, cataloguing approaches from CAD autocompletion and autoconstraint to image-conditioned generation and multimodal text/image/point-cloud-to-CAD pipelines (zhang2026largelanguagemodels pages 16-18, zhang2026largelanguagemodels pages 14-16). Fine-tuned systems such as CAD-Coder achieve 100% valid syntax rates on CadQuery generation from visual input, outperforming GPT-4.5 baselines (doris2026cadcoderanopensource pages 2-3). However, LLM-driven FreeCAD script generation demonstrates clear failure modes: simple primitives succeed on the first attempt, but advanced geometry (involute gear profiles, fully constrained parametric frames) fails to converge within maximum iterations due to unsupported API calls, null shapes, and geometric-constraint challenges (kumar2508generativeaifor pages 4-5, kumar2508generativeaifor pages 3-4). Retrieval-augmented generation substantially improves syntactic correctness and geometric fidelity for OpenSCAD generation, and multi-agent setups help with verification, but geometric accuracy falls with increasing design complexity (kansala2026generativeaidrivensystems pages 62-65). A common execute-refine pipeline sees prompts fed to a VLM/LLM, code executed, and errors or low similarity triggering iterative refinement (zhang2026largelanguagemodels pages 14-16). CADBench uses GPT-4o for image-based and script-based evaluation of generated CAD quality (zhang2026largelanguagemodels pages 16-18).

**Diffusion-based 3D generation.** Models such as Shap-E and Point-E generate 3D assets from text prompts via latent diffusion, producing implicit functions (renderable as meshes and neural radiance fields) or point clouds, respectively (bono2024texttobuildingexperimentswith pages 4-5). Shap-E operates in a latent space of implicit signed-distance functions rather than CAD-native representations, and its outputs are low-resolution (e.g., 16K points) requiring marching-cubes conversion and mesh cleanup before simulation or manufacturing (rios2023largelanguageand pages 2-3). Point-E's two-stage pipeline yields approximately 1,000–4,000 points—"sparse 3D representations" that trade fidelity for speed (hettiarachchi2025textto3daimodeling pages 17-20). These models are promising for conceptual design exploration but do not produce engineering-grade, parametric CAD geometry and need substantial post-processing, remeshing, and validation to meet mechanical-engineering requirements (rios2023largelanguageand pages 2-3, yu2026gencadthreedimensionalcomputeraideddesign pages 12-14).

### 2.3 Systems-Design and Requirements-Driven Approaches

**MBSE and SysML v2.** Model-Based Systems Engineering (MBSE) uses SysML as a formal, general-purpose language providing nine diagram types covering requirements, structure, behaviour, and parametrics (stirgwolt2022amodelbasedsystems pages 4-6). SysML v2 introduces a new metamodel with combined textual and graphical notation and support for structuring models into exchangeable packages (li2024collaborativemodelbasedsystems pages 15-18). Integration with parametric CAD is an active research area: Hoepfner et al. (2023) demonstrate an MBSE approach where SysML parametric descriptions are refined into CAD shell surfaces via dedicated interfaces, though initial CAD models may consist of unconnected geometry requiring further integration (hoepfner2023towardsaholistic pages 9-9). Marvis (2022) shows an MBSE implementation using MagicDraw that links structural analysis outputs to SysML via surrogate-model constraint blocks, wrapping MATLAB scripts to modify 3D geometry and run simulations without leaving the MBSE environment (marvis2022modelbasedsystemsengineering pages 41-45). Daoud et al. (2025) apply the CESAM Systems Architecting method with SysML in CAMEO to optimise additive manufacturing workflows, proposing an advisor system for identifying design flaws and suggesting printability modifications (daoud2025optimizingadditivemanufacturing pages 5-8). However, SysML tools alone are insufficient to fully specify and analyse products, and practical integration relies on bespoke wrappers and model transformations whose maturity and interoperability remain challenging (li2024collaborativemodelbasedsystems pages 1-3, li2024collaborativemodelbasedsystems pages 4-5).

**Design-space exploration and MDO.** OpenMDAO, an open-source multidisciplinary design optimisation framework developed by NASA, provides Newton-type solvers, hierarchical coupled-derivative computation, and modular component-based architecture supporting gradient-based optimisation at scale (gray2019openmdaoanopensource pages 1-2, gray2019openmdaoanopensource pages 2-4, gray2019openmdaoanopensource pages 20-22). It has been applied to trajectory optimisation, wing design, and structural topology optimisation with thousands of design variables (gray2019openmdaoanopensource pages 22-23). WhatsOpt provides a web-based front-end for OpenMDAO, supporting collaborative MDAO workflow definition, design of experiments, Morris-method screening, and surrogate modelling (lafage2019whatsoptaweb pages 13-20, lafage2019whatsoptaweb pages 6-8, lafage2019whatsoptaweb pages 20-21). For smaller-scale hardware problems, black-box optimisers such as Optuna, Nevergrad, and Ax/BoTorch offer practical Bayesian and evolutionary search over CAD parameter spaces without requiring analytic derivatives.

**Self-driving labs for mechanical hardware.** The self-driving laboratory (SDL) concept has been extensively developed for chemistry and materials science. Tom et al. (2024) provide a comprehensive review covering SDL hardware, software, and applications across drug discovery, materials science, and chemistry (deneault2021towardautonomousadditive pages 1-2). The A-Lab at Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory demonstrated fully autonomous solid-state synthesis of 36 inorganic compounds over 17 days of continuous robotic operation, using active learning grounded in thermodynamics to optimise synthesis recipes (deneault2021towardautonomousadditive pages 1-2). However, nearly all published SDLs focus on chemical/materials synthesis rather than geometry-driven mechanical hardware iteration. The gap between chemistry-focused SDLs and mechanical design-build-test loops remains largely unbridged in the literature.

### 2.4 Automated DFM and Printability Checks for Additive Manufacturing

**Slicer-side checks.** Modern slicers (PrusaSlicer, OrcaSlicer, Cura) provide programmatic CLI interfaces and perform implicit DFM checks during slicing: support generation, overhang detection, thin-wall warnings, and build-time/material estimation. These provide practical, production-adjacent feedback but are not designed as standalone DFM analysis tools.

**Open-source DFM tools.** Will It Print is an open-source, MATLAB-based virtual prototyping tool that evaluates STL geometry for compliance with FFF/FDM design-for-additive-manufacturing guidelines (budinoff2021willitprint pages 8-10, budinoff2021willitprint pages 1-2). It performs five core checks: overhang/support detection (flagging facets where the normal angle exceeds a configurable threshold), surface roughness prediction (computing per-facet Ra from the stair-step model), small-feature detection (using multi-scale morphological transforms on voxelised geometry), toppling risk assessment, and warping analysis for large build-plate-contacting areas (budinoff2021willitprint pages 3-5, budinoff2021willitprint pages 5-7, budinoff2021willitprint pages 14-16). Validated experimentally, redesigned parts showed lower scrap rates and improved quality (budinoff2021willitprint pages 1-2, budinoff2021willitprint pages 10-13). The tool is open-source on GitHub, customisable for different AM processes, and planned for a Python port (budinoff2021willitprint pages 13-14, budinoff2021willitprint pages 2-3). Academic work on overhang elimination includes post-topology-optimisation strategies that detect and support infeasible overhanging features via additional struts subject to specified thickness and angle constraints (li2024collaborativemodelbasedsystems pages 19-21).

**Commercial DFM tools.** DFMPro, Castor, and ProtoLabs/Hubs Manufacturing analysers provide professional-grade printability analysis and quoting but require commercial licences and are typically not scriptable for automated loops.

## 3. Topic 2 — Automated Feedback Loops and Digital-Twin Evaluation

### 3.1 Digital Twins for Additively Manufactured Hardware

Kritzinger et al. (2018) establish the foundational taxonomy distinguishing three levels of digital representation: a **Digital Model** permits only manual data exchange with no online synchronisation; a **Digital Shadow** features automated one-way data flow from the physical asset to its virtual counterpart; and a **Digital Twin** entails full, automated, bi-directional data flow enabling the digital object to act as a controlling instance of the physical system (kritzinger2018digitaltwinin pages 1-2, kritzinger2018digitaltwinin pages 2-3). Tao et al. (2019) further elaborate this framework, defining the DT as an integrated, multi-physics, multi-scale simulation using sensor updates to mirror the life of its physical twin (lattanzi2021digitaltwinfor pages 6-9). Lattanzi et al. (2021) note that approximately 85% of practical DT applications target manufacturing devices, while only ~11% address overall process/flow (lattanzi2021digitaltwinfor pages 6-9). An additional "Intelligent Digital Twin" level embeds AI for autonomy, adaptability, and self-awareness (lattanzi2021digitaltwinfor pages 9-11).

In practice, most published AM digital twins operate at the Digital Shadow level—automated data collection from the physical process feeding a virtual model, but without closed-loop control feedback to the physical system. Open-source platforms such as Eclipse Ditto, FIWARE, and the Azure Digital Twins SDKs provide infrastructure for managing digital representations, while ROS2 + Gazebo/Ignition serve mechatronic twin applications. However, achieving a true DT with bidirectional control for lab-scale AM hardware remains rare outside large industrial demonstrators.

### 3.2 Closed-Loop Design → Print → Measure → Re-Design Pipelines

**Bayesian optimisation of printed parts.** Deneault et al. (2021) introduced the Additive Manufacturing Autonomous Research System (AM ARES), a low-cost, open-source research robot that performs fully closed-loop Bayesian optimisation of material-extrusion printing parameters (deneault2021towardautonomousadditive pages 1-2, deneault2021towardautonomousadditive pages 2-3, deneault2021towardautonomousadditive pages 8-9). AM ARES modulates four print parameters (prime delay, print speed, x-offset, y-offset) and uses automated image capture and analysis as direct feedback to an online Bayesian optimiser, learning to print target features in fewer than 100 experiments at 30–60 iterations per hour (deneault2021towardautonomousadditive pages 9-10, deneault2021towardautonomousadditive pages 7-8). Multi-objective Bayesian optimisation (MOBO) extensions further enable simultaneous optimisation of two objectives (myung2025multiobjectivebayesianoptimization pages 1-2). Ganitano et al. (2024) demonstrated autonomous FDM calibration combining computer-vision-based quality analysis with metaheuristic search, achieving submillimetre dimensional accuracy with only 30 g of filament (deneault2021towardautonomousadditive pages 2-3). Sani et al. (2024) review AI-augmented closed-loop 3D printing comprehensively, documenting thermal imaging, multi-sensor CNN classifiers (~94% accuracy), in-line FDM rheometry, and ultrasonic in-process inspection as feedback modalities (sani2024artificialintelligence‐augmentedadditive pages 13-13).

**Self-driving labs touching geometry.** The A-Lab demonstrated autonomous inorganic materials synthesis over 17 days using robotics, ML, and active learning (deneault2021towardautonomousadditive pages 1-2). However, this and nearly all prominent SDLs (Berlinguette/MacLeod's Ada, Aspuru-Guzik group platforms, Cooper group) optimise chemical composition and process parameters rather than part geometry. The gap between chemistry-optimisation SDLs and geometry-optimisation SDLs remains a frontier opportunity.

### 3.3 Automated Functional and Performance Evaluation

**CV-based dimensional metrology.** Photogrammetry pipelines based on COLMAP, Meshroom, and RealityCapture can reconstruct 3D point clouds from multi-view images for comparison against nominal CAD geometry. Structured-light scanners (GOM ATOS, Polyga) provide higher-fidelity dimensional data for automated GD&T comparison. For small AM parts, turntable photogrammetry with COLMAP provides a cost-effective path to sub-millimetre surface reconstruction.

**Open-source automated test platforms.** Jubilee is an open-source, tool-changing motion platform that supports modular end-effectors for liquid handling, imaging, syringe dispensing, and custom tools (subbaraman2024theduckbota pages 5-7, pelkie2025automationandautonomous pages 90-94). Built from commercially available components with a base kit cost of approximately $1,700, Jubilee uses a Duet3 controller and Raspberry Pi, exposes G-code and Python (Science-Jubilee) interfaces, and is controlled from Jupyter notebooks (subbaraman2024theduckbota pages 5-7, pelkie2025automationandautonomous pages 98-102, pelkie2025automationandautonomous pages 94-98). It has been deployed for autonomous colour-matching Bayesian optimisation, sonochemical nanocrystal synthesis, and duckweed growth assays (pelkie2025democratizingselfdrivinglabs pages 9-11, politi2024opensciencematerialsacceleration pages 169-174, politi2024opensciencematerialsacceleration pages 165-169, politi2024opensciencematerialsacceleration pages 189-193). The Science-Jubilee Python package mirrors OpenTrons API conventions and provides tool-specific modules, deck layout management, and calibration notebooks (pelkie2025automationandautonomous pages 98-102, pelkie2025democratizingselfdrivinglabs pages 9-11). Recommended upgrades for production SDL use include additional sensors for state verification, chemically compatible metal/resin parts, spill containment, and a digital twin for offline protocol testing (politi2024opensciencematerialsacceleration pages 189-193).

### 3.4 A Credible End-to-End Feedback Loop for the Powder-Excavator

For our specific use case—a passive half-cylinder trough that scoops and tips dozens-of-micron inorganic powders via a sawtooth ledge—a credible automated feedback loop would proceed as follows:

1. **Parameterise** the trough geometry in CadQuery/build123d: trough radius, lip angle, wall thickness, pivot-pin offset, sawtooth engagement depth, and stop geometry as ~6–10 continuous parameters.
2. **Generate** STEP and STL exports programmatically; run DFM checks via a PrusaSlicer CLI call (support volume, estimated print time) and optionally Will It Print (overhang, warping, small-feature flags).
3. **Print** on an FDM/SLS printer (PETG or nylon), potentially with automated bed-clearing if using a belt printer or robotic arm.
4. **Test** on a Jubilee/Science-Jubilee rig configured with: (a) a syringe or hopper tool to load a known powder charge, (b) a precision balance (0.001 g) under the deposition zone, (c) a top-down camera for OpenCV-based pile-shape analysis (area, circularity, spread uniformity as proxies for dose CV).
5. **Evaluate** delivered mass, mass CV across N trials, pile footprint, and residue on the trough; log environmental variables (humidity, temperature).
6. **Optimise** using Ax/BoTorch (Bayesian optimisation with noisy observations, multi-objective if needed) or Optuna; feed new parameter vector back to step 1.

**Critique of this sketch.** The main risks are: (a) powder behaviour is dominated by humidity, electrostatics, and cleaning history, so environmental logging and cleaning protocols must be tightly controlled or the optimiser will fit noise; (b) the print-test cycle time (~hours per iteration) limits the practical number of iterations, making sample-efficient Bayesian optimisation essential; (c) the balance + vision feedback must be calibrated and validated to ensure the measured objectives actually correlate with dosing quality; (d) the Jubilee platform's positioning repeatability (~50 µm) is adequate for this application but would need verification for fine powder metering; (e) an automated bed-swap or part-ejection mechanism is needed to close the loop fully without human intervention between prints.

## 4. Recommended Toolchain for the Powder-Excavator Project

Based on the literature synthesis above, we recommend the following concrete toolchain:

| Generative exploration | Optuna + CadQuery | For this project, “generative” should mean structured design-space exploration over trough radius, lip angle, pivot offset, wall thickness, stop geometry, and print orientation, rather than unconstrained AI shape synthesis; Optuna gives practical black-box optimization and can wrap deterministic CAD generation and downstream metrics (lafage2019whatsoptaweb pages 13-20, lafage2019whatsoptaweb pages 6-8, gray2019openmdaoanopensource pages 20-22) | Ax/BoTorch or Nevergrad if multi-objective Bayesian optimization becomes central; commercial topology tools if structural mass reduction becomes important later (gray2019openmdaoanopensource pages 20-22, lafage2019whatsoptaweb pages 20-21, li2024collaborativemodelbasedsystems pages 19-21, li2024collaborativemodelbasedsystems pages 15-18) | Optimizer performance depends on good parameterization and measurable objectives; if the CAD parameter set is poorly chosen, optimization will search the wrong design space |
| DFM/printability check | PrusaSlicer CLI + Will It Print | PrusaSlicer gives practical, production-adjacent slicing outputs, support/orientation estimates, and g-code generation, while Will It Print adds explicit geometry-oriented FDM checks for overhangs, warping, toppling, surface roughness, and small features; together they cover both slicer realism and explicit DFM heuristics (budinoff2021willitprint pages 8-10, budinoff2021willitprint pages 1-2, budinoff2021willitprint pages 3-5, budinoff2021willitprint pages 14-16, budinoff2021willitprint pages 10-13, budinoff2021willitprint pages 5-7) | OrcaSlicer or Cura in place of PrusaSlicer; commercial DFM analyzers if supplier-facing quoting/manufacturing checks become necessary | Toolchain split between slicer and separate DFM logic can create duplicated or inconsistent orientation judgments; Will It Print is MATLAB-based and may need wrapper work for seamless automation (budinoff2021willitprint pages 13-14) |
| Prototype feedback loop | Jubilee / Science Jubilee platform + precision balance + OpenCV + Ax/BoTorch | A gantry-style, repeatable benchtop automation platform is well matched to a passive powder-handling test rig: Jubilee already supports modular toolchanging and laboratory automation, while a precision balance plus vision can quantify delivered mass, pile shape, residue, and failure events; Ax/BoTorch can close the loop on noisy, expensive experiments (subbaraman2024theduckbota pages 5-7, pelkie2025automationandautonomous pages 98-102, pelkie2025automationandautonomous pages 90-94, pelkie2025democratizingselfdrivinglabs pages 9-11, politi2024opensciencematerialsacceleration pages 169-174, politi2024opensciencematerialsacceleration pages 165-169, politi2024opensciencematerialsacceleration pages 189-193, pelkie2025automationandautonomous pages 94-98, deneault2021towardautonomousadditive pages 1-2, deneault2021towardautonomousadditive pages 9-10, deneault2021towardautonomousadditive pages 7-8) | OpenTrons if liquid-handling style infrastructure is preferred, or a custom CoreXY rig if powder mechanics require nonstandard fixturing | Powder behavior may be dominated by humidity, electrostatics, and cleaning history, so sensorized repeatability and environmental logging are essential; otherwise the optimizer will fit noise rather than design effects |
| Integration/wiring | Python + Jupyter | Python is the common denominator across CadQuery/build123d, optimization libraries, machine vision, data logging, and lab automation, and Jupyter is the lowest-effort environment for an auditable design-build-test notebook workflow that can later be hardened into scripts/CI (guan2025cadcodertexttocadgeneration pages 14-16, lafage2019whatsoptaweb pages 13-20, subbaraman2024theduckbota pages 5-7, politi2024opensciencematerialsacceleration pages 189-193) | Pure Python package/CLI pipeline without notebooks for stricter reproducibility, or OpenMDAO if the workflow evolves into a larger multidisciplinary optimization stack (lafage2019whatsoptaweb pages 13-20, gray2019openmdaoanopensource pages 1-2, gray2019openmdaoanopensource pages 2-4, gray2019openmdaoanopensource pages 22-23) | Notebook-centric workflows can become fragile and poorly versioned unless parameter definitions, run metadata, and artifact export are disciplined from the start |


*Table: This table summarizes a concrete low-friction toolchain for the powder-excavator project, covering CAD authoring, generative exploration, DFM, automated testing, and integration. It is useful because it translates the literature survey into a practical adoption decision with clear alternatives and risks.*

The lowest-effort path to wire these together is a single Python monorepo containing: (i) a CadQuery/build123d parametric model of the trough/ledge assembly, (ii) a `generate_and_check.py` script that sweeps parameters, exports STEP/STL, calls PrusaSlicer CLI for slicing metrics, and logs DFM flags, (iii) an Optuna or Ax study definition that wraps the generate-print-test cycle, (iv) a Jupyter notebook for interactive exploration and visualisation of the design space, and (v) Science-Jubilee control scripts for the physical test rig. This architecture keeps all tooling in the Python ecosystem, version-controllable in the project's GitHub repository, and amenable to incremental automation from manual-first to fully closed-loop operation.
